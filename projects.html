Project Overview

Bootlegger Bust was developed as part of a six-week collaborative assignment where we were tasked with designing a fully playable first-person shooter experience in Unreal Engine 5. The goal was to create a visually compelling and mechanically engaging environment using only free, publicly available assets (such as those from the Unreal Marketplace or Quixel Megascans). Working within this constraint encouraged creativity, efficient design practices, and smart use of available resources while maintaining a professional level of polish. As the team's primary level designer, I focused on layout, flow, player decision-making, and atmosphere to bring our vision to life.

Bootlegger Bust

Goals & Constraints

Project Goals:

  • Design a fully playable first-person shooter (FPS) level lasting between 2–5 minutes.
  • Build the environment in Unreal Engine using only pre-made assets from FAB.
  • Incorporate verticality in level layout to encourage varied player navigation and spatial awareness.
  • Expand basic player movement by implementing mechanics such as jumping and crouching.
  • Include health and pickup systems, along with a user interface (UI) to display player status.

Production Constraints:

  • A tight development timeline of approximately six weeks from initial planning to final presentation.
  • Team members balancing this project alongside other coursework, responsibilities, and part-time jobs.
  • Conflicting opinions on level design arose often, making compromise essential throughout this process.
  • The majority of the team, including myself, are beginners in video game design, making this project a valuable learning experience amid new challenges.

Concept & Inspiration

The level began with a digital top-down layout that mapped out major rooms, pathways, and player objectives. This early phase helped guide the spatial flow and gameplay rhythm. The visual direction was heavily inspired by 1920s Prohibition-era crime settings, particularly the style and atmosphere of classic Italian mob films. We referenced real-world “mafia boss” rooms and vintage industrial interiors to inform our environment. While not a stealth game, the level embraces a darker, moodier tone, with warm lighting and layered detail meant to evoke tension and cinematic style.

Whitebox / Blockout

As the lead level designer, I was responsible for whiteboxing all three of the level’s core rooms. My focus during this phase was on creating intuitive gameplay spaces by carefully planning out player pathways, obstacles, and strategic cover placement. A major design priority was crafting enemy encounter zones that felt challenging yet fair, with layouts that encouraged tactical movement and moment-to-moment decision making. I also paid close attention to size and scale to ensure the spaces felt grounded and believable. Throughout the process, I applied key level design principles—such as framing focal points and using light to subtly guide the player—to support both navigation and visual storytelling.

Final Renderings

In the final stage, the entire team collaborated closely to setdress the environment, refine collectible placements, and tweak enemy positions to enhance gameplay pacing and player experience. Lighting was a significant focus, carefully crafted to reinforce the moody, atmospheric tone of the Prohibition-era setting. While I was not deeply involved in developing the core gameplay mechanics and systems themselves, I maintained a strong understanding of their structure and integration within the level to ensure design cohesion. This phase was crucial in bringing polish and visual storytelling to the world, making it feel immersive and engaging.

Conclusion & Learnings

  • Understanding the importance of early layout planning to ensure smooth gameplay flow and pacing.
  • Applying core level design principles like framing and lighting to guide players intuitively without explicit instructions.
  • Balancing challenge and fun through careful enemy placement and strategic cover design.
  • Collaborating effectively within a team of beginners, learning how to integrate art, gameplay, and technical elements.
  • Gaining practical experience with Unreal Engine’s tools for whiteboxing, lighting, and environment set dressing.
  • Recognizing the value of iteration and playtesting to refine level flow and player experience.
  • Learning to manage scope and expectations within a limited timeline and resources.
  • Developing a stronger understanding of how level design fits within overall game design and production pipelines.
